Your organization requires two factor authentication. After internal users enter their username and password they are required to enter an 8 digit continually changing security code displayed on their smart card. A senior manager wants to allow the help desk to bypass the second factor if the employee calls up, states that they do not have the smart card currently, and “satisfies” the agent through personal questions that they are who they say they are? Make both cases for and against this protocol.

There have been cases where I have forgotten a credential that I had to have in order to get in.  It's very embarressing to have to ask somebody authoritative for a temporary credemtoal to get into a system.  It's even more embarressing to go home and be unable to find it, only to discover that you'd put it in a different pocket than what you were expecting - you had it all along.  That second credential authenticates you, but there are other ways to authenticate and it isn't clear to me that just because the organization has selected a specific two doesn't mean that that should be the only two.  Also, being hard nosed about such things can lead to people "sharing" username/password pairs, which is even worse.

On the other hand, asking somebody to vouch for you can be problematic.  The person vouching can be fooled.  If the person doing the vouching has been compromised somehow, then they have allowed a non-authentic person to have access to a system.  People have been warned, and they should take precautions to make sure they have the tools they need to do their job.  One or two instances of this should make one most careful for the rest of their career.

The global positioning system was developed by the US military towards the end of the 20th century and for a while gave them a significant tactical advantage. Nevertheless the US government opened up the GPS system to the entire world. Provide the pros and cons of this from the perspective of a cyber security professional.

GPS gave the US DoD a tactical advantage by degrading itself in times of war to parties which didn't have the magic number to get the high accuracy data.  This was defeated.  The adversaries would measure a known location with high accuracy.  When war came, the adversary would measure where GPS thought the known point was.  Then they would subtract where GPS thought the point was from where it actually was and get a difference vector.  Then everybody in the theater would know what GPS was deliberately doing wrong, and would compensate for it.  So the US Government opened GPS to the world.

And why not?  Opening GPS didn't increase the cost of the system, it just meant that there was a switch that would get never turned on anymore.  Having the world use GPS also generated some good will for the US Government.


Compare and contrast the “kill chain®[Lockheed/Martin]” vs the "ATT&CK framework [Mitre]". Which do you prefer and why?

The Mitre ATT&CK framework has 14 columns, and 218 cells.  That's too big to be kept in one's head.  By way of contrast,  the kill chain® tends to sweep all of those details all into a single check box, and that means those details are out of view.  I guess it depends on whether one is a "big picture" thinker or somebody who is minutely interested in details.  The kill chain® has a note of optimism that I really like: Stopping adversaries at any stage breaks the chain of attack!(I downloaded Gaining_the_Advantage_Cyber_Kill_Chain.pdf)

The way I tested these two frameworks was with a hypothetical: A bad guy bribes the janitor into placing a little gizmo that plugs into the wired network.  There several ways to think about this scenario:

  • It's a physical access issue - sort of.  If the bad guy could pick a lock, then definitely would be physical access.  I am willing to entertain that this is a physical issue because the little gizmo has to be placed inside the facility, so any security mechanism that looks for external attacks is going to miss it.
  • It's definitely a human problem - the janitors should not be bribable.
  • There is the technical problem that a previously unknown MAC address has shown up on the network.  This aspect manifests itself in the Discovery column of the Mitre ATT&CK framework and the reconnaissance and delivery stages of the kill chain framework.


Data exfiltration presents a significant challenge for DLP programs. Discuss why it is so hard and various means to manage its risk.

There lots of ways of getting information out by manipulating the network in ways that violate conventions but still work. For example, you don't have to use HTTP over port 80: you could run a TLS over TCP connection with your own protocol. It would look like a web connection unless your proxy had the compute power to do some really deep packet inspection.


As US Secretary of Defense, Donald Rumsfeld achieved significant notice and some notoriety for espousing the “unknown unknown” concept. While he applied it in geopolitics, cybersecurity professionals respect it as well. Discuss strategies for managing the risk of “unknown unknowns” and provide a recent example of one if you can.

I can think of at least two kinds, and I am going to limit my discussion to open source software.

  1. Vulnerabilities in open source software e.g. heartbleed and log4j.  In both of these cases the vulnerability was introduced by accident.
  2. Malware hiding in open source libraries.  See Malware downloaded from PyPI 41,000 times was surprisingly stealthy.  I learned about this from a presentation given at pycascades 2022, February 6th, by Gajendra Deshpande, Security considerations in Python Packaging. This is a supply chain attack, just as the Solar Winds attack was, the only real difference being that it was much smaller (tens of thousands of downloads, compared with millions) and it is easier to mitigate.

In both cases, it really didn't occur to us that problems of such magnitude would escape detection as the code went through the release process.  In the case of heartbleed, the code was so complicated that it can be understood by perhaps a dozen people on the entire planet.  In the case of Log4j, the error was so obvious that nobody looking at it recognized it as an error.  In the cases of the malware in PyPi, nobody is responsible for the content in the library.  People who use the code that comes out of the library are responsible for detecting any security problems, but of course, nobody ever does that.  Sunday at the pycascades convention, I leared about 3 tools for inspecting python modules from PyPi: bandit, safety, and semgrep (an open source static analyzer).

Diamond Model of Intrusion analysis:

  1. Victim Discovers Malware
  2. Malware contains C2 domain
  3. C2 domain resolves to C2 IP address
  4. Firewall logs reveal further victims contacting C2 IP address.  (Wirewall logs outbound IP address references)
  5. IP address ownership reveal adversary.
